Breaking the Silence: Confronting Gender-Based Violence in Lesotho

Gender-based violence (GBV) in Lesotho remains one of the most pressing social challenges, deeply rooted in cultural norms and economic inequalities. Studies show that more than half of Basotho women have experienced some form of violence in their lifetime, a rate significantly higher than the global average. Patriarchal traditions and entrenched gender roles often perpetuate the idea that men hold authority over women, reinforcing cycles of abuse. Poverty and unemployment further exacerbate the problem, leaving many women economically dependent and vulnerable to exploitation and violence.

Public perception reflects the severity of the issue. According to Afrobarometer, a majority of Basotho citizens identify GBV as the most urgent women’s rights issue that needs to be addressed. More than half of respondents reported that violence against women is either “somewhat common” or “very common” in their communities. Encouragingly, most Basotho reject the notion that men are justified in physically disciplining their wives, though a minority still believe it is acceptable under certain circumstances. This highlights both progress in shifting attitudes and the persistence of harmful beliefs that sustain GBV.

Efforts to combat GBV in Lesotho have gained momentum in recent years, with government initiatives, civil society organizations, and international partners working together to strengthen legal protections and raise awareness. For example, agreements between unions, NGOs, and employers in the textile industry have sought to eliminate workplace harassment and violence, offering remedies and accountability mechanisms. These initiatives, while promising, require sustained commitment and broader societal change to dismantle the structural inequalities that fuel GBV. Addressing the issue holistically through education, economic empowerment, and stronger enforcement of laws remains essential for building a safer and more equitable society.
